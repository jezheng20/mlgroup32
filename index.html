Introduction/Background

With the ubiquitousness of the Internet today, accessibility has become undeniably more important, yet harder to accommodate. A large number of websites, like Wikipedia, present images that serve as an important visual supplement to the other information on that site; everyone who uses the Internet relies on viewing images to glean knowledge online. However, due to the sheer volume of websites and material currently available to the general public, it is quite unlikely that the majority of images online are completely accessible. Accessibility tools, especially those which focus on improving the process of captioning images, are therefore important because they advocate for the inclusion of socially disadvantaged individuals and people with disabilities using the Internet.

Through the development of a machine learning tool that can classify and caption images, we strive to contribute to the task of increasing the overall accessibility of online knowledge with regards specifically to image recognition and identification.

Problem definition

Wikipedia articles feature many images in addition to the content that they present, but most of the images featured within these Wikipedia articles have no written context associated with them [1]. This is an issue because not everyone who is using and reading online articles featuring images will be able to access these images equally. The ability to access and read image captions depends on multiple social and economic factors and disadvantages.

To combat this problem, tools such as “alt text” and captions have come into play, but a large number of accessibility lacking images still remain. We aim to create a model that will supply captions for images on a wide variety of subjects and scenarios and through this we will be able to make the internet a little more accessible. Specifically, through the creation of such a model this semester, our goal is to develop a model that will be able to identify and potentially caption pictures of animals and humans, mainly. We additionally are interested in adding features to the tool so that we may identify and caption mythical creatures such as dragons, mermaids, and werewolves involved in various actions!

Our main testing data came from a Kaggle [2] dataset, gathered from Flickr, that contains more than eight thousand images accompanied with captions. These images cover a range of subjects from humans, to dogs, cats and other animals, performing all different actions. Each image is accompanied by a caption, which, of course, describes the subject of the image. An example of this can be seen below.

The captions, though relatively simple, still provide ample detail of what's occurring in the image, for example: “​​A girl going into a wooden building” and “A black dog and a spotted dog are fighting”.

We also found a dataset of medium resolution animal images on Kaggle [3] to supplement our other general image and caption one. This dataset contains only ten different types of animals, however we believe it will still be useful as it includes common types of animals like dogs, cats, horses, sheep and more.

Methods

The main modeling technique we decided to use to solve our problem is by gathering image features using a convolutional net (CNN) and feeding this, along with the output from long short-term memory (LSTM), into a combined model. This type of model would work best, as LSTMs are good at sequence prediction of data due their recursive properties; this fits perfectly for our captions, which are sequences of words. The LSTM alone, however, cannot discern image features, hence the CNN.

We fed the images through CNN to get the important information, also known as features, from each image. This results in an array with a list of features for each image – for the VGG, this was 4096 features per image, for the ResNet, this was 2048.

From there, we first prepared the list of captions via a method known as tokenization: we converted each word in the caption into its corresponding index in a dictionary. To allow the LSTM to actually predict a word given the sequence of letters before it, we split the data into segments before and after each word:

“The quick brown fox” would be split into input and expected output:

“The” → “quick brown fox”,

“The quick” → “brown fox”

“The quick brown” → “fox”.

In reality, we also added words labeled “<start>” and “<end>” to the beginning and ends of each sequence, indicating the starts and ends of each sequence to tell the LSTM when to end its recursive generation. Also, the sequences were padded to be the same length.

With the tokenizer full of caption keywords, we trained the LSTM model on our data via supervised learning, using [image_features, sequence] as input and [expected sequence] as output.

We experimented with multiple feature extraction models; in addition to using VGG16, we experimented by extracting features using ResNet50. VGG gives 4096 features per image, meaning the LSTM had to take an input vector of length 4096. ResNet outputs 2048 features, which, though less descriptive, meant less parameters and thus less training time for the LSTM part of our model; while VGG required around 42 minutes per epoch, ResNet required 37.

Overall, both models could tell what the subject of the photo was; e.g. man, woman, child, dog – both had a special knack for discerning dogs. However, both was often inaccurate in actually distinguishing the actions in the photo.

Sometimes, the models generated some strange sequences; the LSTM did not properly end the sequence using an <end> token, resulting in a caption with the max length of 34. We believe that this is a quirk which will be mitigated with further training.

It is extremely important to note that the ResNet50 features actually performed better than the VGG features, having lower losses, higher accuracies, and lower entropy with the ground truth – an indication of higher overall caption quality. This shows that a lot of the VGG16 features were either useless or distracting, resulting in a slight drop in caption quality.
